  ---
  Challenges to the Spec

  1. The win condition is a lie

  You defined a win condition (fill all 64 cells), but let's do the math. At ~40
   points, the snake occupies 43 of 64 cells at 7 ticks/sec. The player is
  navigating a snake covering 67% of an 8x8 board at high speed. The last 20
  points require near-perfect pathing at 7-9 ticks/sec on a board so small
  there's almost no room to think. This isn't a goal — it's a decoration. Nobody
   will win. So why pretend?

  The uncomfortable alternative: Drop the win condition. Call it what it is — a
  survival game. "Your score was 34." Done. OR: make the board bigger (12x12,
  16x16) so the win condition is actually a reachable aspiration.

  Counter-counter: Maybe the unreachable win is the point. The joy is in trying.
   But then don't put a win screen in the spec — put a "if by some miracle you
  fill the board, something delightful happens" note.

  2. Stars are a trap, not a reward

  Late-game, a star is poison. At 60 cells, eating a star (+3 growth) means your
   snake hits 63 in 3 ticks. On tick 3, you MUST eat the apple or you die (every
   non-apple cell is your body). If the apple isn't reachable in exactly the
  right position at exactly the right tick — instant death. The player is
  punished for picking up what looks like a bonus.

  Early game? Stars are free points with zero risk. So the "decision" is
  trivially obvious: eat stars early, dodge stars late. That's not interesting
  gameplay, it's a knowledge check.

  The uncomfortable alternative: Stars should be worth risk at ALL stages.
  Maybe: star doesn't add growth, it gives bonus points only (say 5 points,
  triggering speed increases faster). Now it's a trade — take the star for
  points but accelerate the game. OR: star temporarily makes you invincible
  (pass through your own body for 5 ticks). Now it's always valuable.

  3. The speed curve is backwards

  3 ticks/sec at the start is glacial. On an 8x8 board, crossing takes 2.7
  seconds. The player is bored for the first minute. Then at 30 points: 6
  ticks/sec, snake is 33 cells (half the board), and it's suddenly frantic. The
  difficulty doesn't ramp — it cliff-dives.

  The uncomfortable alternative: Start at 5 ticks/sec (immediately engaging) and
   increase by 0.5/10pts. The ceiling is lower, the floor is higher, the curve
  is smoother. OR: don't increase speed at all. The shrinking free space IS the
  difficulty curve. Adding speed on top of shrinking space is double-punishing.

  4. The queue depth of 3 breaks the game on an 8x8 board

  3 queued moves = 3 pre-planned ticks. At 3 ticks/sec, that's a full second of
  pre-programmed movement — nearly half the board width. The player isn't
  reacting, they're scripting. At higher speeds this gets worse: the queue
  represents a smaller time window but the same spatial distance.

  The uncomfortable alternative: Queue depth 2. Or even 1 (just current + next).
   The fun of snake is reacting, not pre-planning. A 3-deep queue makes the game
   more forgiving but less exciting.

  5. "Ignore key repeats" is underspecified and possibly wrong

  Pygame fires KEYDOWN for both fresh presses and OS-generated repeats. The
  events are identical. The only way to detect repeats is to track KEYUP and
  reject KEYDOWN for keys that haven't been released. That's not hard but it's a
   real implementation constraint that should be in the spec.

  But more importantly: should you ignore them? If I'm holding right and I press
   down, I want to turn down. If the "right" repeat arrives just before my
  "down" press and fills the queue... I can't turn. The repeat-ignore logic must
   be per-key, not global. And it must not block inputs from OTHER keys. This
  needs a precise rule:

  A KEYDOWN event for key K is ignored if K has not received a KEYUP since the 
  last KEYDOWN for K.

  6. Tail-chase legality makes the game trivially solvable

  If you can move into the cell your tail is vacating, a perfect Hamiltonian
  cycle strategy exists: follow a fixed path that visits all 64 cells, chasing
  your own tail forever. On an 8x8 board, this path is well-known (zigzag). A
  player who discovers this never dies. The game becomes a solved exercise, not
  a challenge.

  The uncomfortable alternative: Make tail-chase ILLEGAL. Now the snake can't
  follow a Hamiltonian cycle. The player has to actually navigate, take risks,
  and occasionally get boxed in. The game becomes about managing space, not
  memorizing a pattern.

  Counter: Hamiltonian cycle play is boring. Nobody will do it for fun. But the
  spec is about what's POSSIBLE, not what's likely. If you're speccing a game
  with a win condition, you need to decide: is the game meant to be solvable?

  7. No pause is hostile

  You said "built for joy." Then you denied pause. What happens when the
  doorbell rings? When the cat jumps on the keyboard? The player loses a game
  they were enjoying because life happened. That's not joyful — it's punishing.

  The uncomfortable alternative: Add pause. Space bar. It costs nothing,
  prevents frustration, and respects the player's time. "No pause" is a design
  choice from arcade games that wanted more quarters. You're not charging
  quarters.
